Question 1 — Sampling (2 pts)
A university wants to study students' sleep habits. They email a survey to all 8,000 students. Only 1,200 respond. Of these, 60% report sleeping less than 7 hours.

a) What type of sampling is this?

b) What type of bias is most likely present?

c) They want to estimate the proportion with margin of error M=0.03 at 95% confidence. What sample size is needed? (Use π=0.5 if unknown)

Solution

a) This is a voluntary response — they sent it to everyone but only those who chose to respond did. It's NOT an SRS because not everyone had an equal chance of being in the sample (self-selection).

b) Nonresponse bias — only 1,200/8,000 (15%) responded. Those who respond likely differ from non-responders (e.g., students with sleep problems may be more motivated to respond).

c) n = π(1-π)(z*/M)² = 0.5 × 0.5 × (1.96/0.03)² = 0.25 × 4268.44 = 1067.11 → n = 1068 (always round UP)

Question 2 — Test Selection (2 pts)
For each scenario, select the appropriate statistical test:

a) A teacher measures 25 students' anxiety levels before and after an exam.

b) A poll finds 45% of 500 men and 52% of 600 women support a policy. Is the difference significant?

c) Does adding "years of experience" to a salary model that already includes "education level" significantly improve prediction?

d) Is there an association between favorite OS (Windows/Mac/Linux) and study program (CS/AI/DS)?

Solution

a) Paired t-test — SAME 25 students measured at two time points (before/after). Compute difference for each student, then one-sample t-test on differences.

b) z-test for 2 proportions — Two independent groups (men/women), categorical response (support yes/no), comparing two proportions.

c) F-test (nested models) — Comparing two nested regression models. The smaller model is nested within the larger one. Use F = ((SSE₁-SSE₂)/(k₂-k₁)) / (SSE₂/(n-(k₂+1))).

d) Chi-squared test of independence — Two categorical variables (OS and program), 3×3 table. Test if they're independent.

Question 3 — Regression (2.5 pts)
A model predicts exam score from study hours and attendance (n=80):
ŷ = 32.1 + 4.7·(hours) + 0.85·(attendance%)

R² = 0.68, s = 8.2, Total SS = 16400

For hours: b = 4.7, se = 1.2  |  For attendance: b = 0.85, se = 0.31

a) Interpret the slope of "hours" in context:

b) What is the Residual SS (SSE)?

c) Test whether study hours is a significant predictor at α = 0.05. State the t-statistic, df, and conclusion.

d) Calculate the F-statistic for the overall model and state the degrees of freedom.

Solution

a) For each additional hour of study, holding attendance constant, the predicted exam score increases by 4.7 points.

b) R² = 1 - SSE/Total SS → 0.68 = 1 - SSE/16400 → SSE = 16400 × (1 - 0.68) = 5248

c) t = b/se = 4.7/1.2 = 3.917, df = n-(k+1) = 80-3 = 77. Since |t| = 3.917 is very large (p-value ≪ 0.05), we reject H₀. There is sufficient evidence that study hours is a significant predictor of exam score, holding attendance constant.

d) F = (R²/k) / ((1-R²)/(n-(k+1))) = (0.68/2) / (0.32/77) = 0.34 / 0.004156 = 81.8, df₁ = 2, df₂ = 77. This is highly significant — the model explains significant variability.

Question 4 — Hypothesis Test (2 pts)
A company claims 40% of its users prefer dark mode. In a random sample of 200 users, 92 preferred dark mode. Test at α = 0.05 whether the true proportion is higher than 40%.

Work through all 5 steps:

Solution

Step 1 — Assumptions: Random sample ✓. nπ₀ = 200(0.40) = 80 ≥ 15 ✓. n(1-π₀) = 200(0.60) = 120 ≥ 15 ✓.

Step 2 — Hypotheses: H₀: π = 0.40 vs Hₐ: π > 0.40 (one-sided, right-tailed)

Step 3 — Test statistic: p̂ = 92/200 = 0.46. z = (0.46 - 0.40) / √(0.40×0.60/200) = 0.06 / √(0.0012) = 0.06 / 0.03464 = 1.732

Step 4 — P-value: P(Z > 1.732) ≈ 0.0416 (from Table A)

Step 5 — Conclusion: Since p-value (0.0416) < α (0.05), we reject H₀. There is sufficient evidence at the 5% significance level that the proportion of users preferring dark mode is greater than 40%.
